# Skotino Cave, the Labyrinth, and Britomartis

## Overview

Paul Faure's *Fonctions des Cavernes Crétoises* (1964) proposes that the **Cave of Skotino** (Agia Paraskevi)—not the Palace of Knossos—was the original Labyrinth: a vast limestone cavern with four descending levels, worked stalagmites resembling human and animal forms, and continuous cult deposits from the Middle Minoan period through Byzantine times. Linear B tablet Gg 702 records offerings to the **da-pu₂-ri-to-jo po-ti-ni-ja** ("Mistress of the Labyrinth"), and Faure identifies this goddess with **Britomartis**—the pre-Greek "Sweet Maiden" (βριτός + μάρτις) worshipped at nearby Chersonesos.

The Britomartis tradition preserves key elements for understanding Minoan religion. Callimachus records that **Minos pursued Britomartis for nine months** across Crete, inverting the typical theogamic pattern where a god pursues a mortal woman. This pursuit encodes either sacred marriage ritual or the transition from matrilineal goddess-centered religion to patriarchal kingship. The goddess leaps into the sea, is saved by fishermen's nets (hence her epithet **Diktynna**, "Lady of the Nets"), and is elevated to divine status by Artemis. Pausanias records that **Daedalus crafted her cult statue at Olous**—connecting the master craftsman directly to the Labyrinth goddess.

Faure's four-fold typology of Cretan cave cults—military initiation, child-rearing (kourotrophy), pastoral rites, and chthonic/agricultural worship—provides a framework for understanding how the Labyrinth functioned as a sanctuary complex where different priesthoods conducted specialized rites. The cave, not the palace, was the "House of the Double Axe" where initiates descended into darkness to emerge transformed.

## Key Arguments

### 1. Faure's Skotino-Labyrinth Thesis

**The core argument** (Faure 1964, p. 169):

> "Il y a donc lieu de chercher au voisinage de Knosos une caverne sacrée minoenne où l'on retrouve un culte de grande déesse, un ensemble de galeries complexes, une figure du Minotaure, des traces de travail artistique, des aires de danse. Je constate, après l'examen de toutes les cavernes de culte et d'un grand nombre d'autres entre les flancs ouest du Lasithi et les flancs est de l'Ida, limites de la plus grande extension de l'État Knosien, que, **seule, la caverne de Skotino répond à toutes ces exigences.**"
>
> (There is therefore reason to search in the vicinity of Knossos for a sacred Minoan cave where one finds a cult of the great goddess, a complex of galleries, a figure of the Minotaur, traces of artistic work, dance floors. I observe, after examining all the cult caves and many others between the western flanks of Lasithi and the eastern flanks of Ida, limits of the greatest extension of the Knossian State, that **only the cave of Skotino meets all these requirements.**)

**Physical characteristics of Skotino**:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Depth** | 55m vertical drop through four levels |
| **Structure** | Complex of galleries and chambers |
| **Formations** | Worked stalagmites resembling human/animal forms |
| **Cult deposits** | MM through Roman period (continuous) |
| **Dance floors** | Open areas at entrance (festivals still held July 26) |

**Linear B attestations**:
- KN X 140: **da-pu₂-ri-to** (probable reference to labyrinth)
- KN Gg 702: **da-pu₂-ri-to-jo po-ti-ni-ja** ("Mistress of the Labyrinth")

### 2. Britomartis as the Labyrinth Goddess

**Faure's identification** (p. 165):

> "J'ai proposé, en 1958, le nom de Britomarpis comme celui, possible, de la déesse de Skotino, parce qu'elle était la déesse principale de Khersonasos, la ville la plus proche de la caverne."
>
> (I proposed in 1958 the name Britomarpis as the possible [name] of the goddess of Skotino, because she was the principal goddess of Chersonesos, the city closest to the cave.)

**Name etymology**:

| Element | Meaning | Evidence |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **βριτό-** (*britos*) | Sweet, blessing | Pre-Greek substrate |
| **-μαρτις** (*martis*) | Maiden | Related to virgin goddess type |
| **Δίκτυννα** (*Diktynna*) | Of the nets | Secondary epithet from escape myth |

**The Daedalus connection** (Pausanias 9.39.3):
Daedalus crafted a wooden cult statue of Britomartis at **Olous**—placing the legendary craftsman in direct service to the Labyrinth goddess.

### 3. The Nine-Month Pursuit Myth

**Callimachus, *Hymn to Artemis* 188ff**:

> "And beyond others thou [Artemis] lovest the Nymphe of Gortyn [in Krete], Britomartis, slayer of stags, the goodly archer; for love of whom was Minos of old distraught and roamed the hills of Krete. And for **nine months** he roamed over crag and cliff and made not an end of pursuing, until, all but caught, she leapt into the sea from the top of a cliff and fell into the **nets of fishermen** which saved her."

**Structural analysis**:

| Element | Normal Theogamy | Britomartis Myth |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| Pursuer | God (Zeus) | Mortal king (Minos) |
| Pursued | Mortal woman | Goddess |
| Duration | Unspecified | Nine months (gestation period) |
| Outcome | Union | Escape/apotheosis |

**Interpretation**: The myth inverts the typical divine pursuit pattern. This may encode:
- **Sacred marriage** ritual where the king "pursues" the goddess
- **Religious transition** from goddess-centered to king-centered cult
- **Gestation symbolism** (9 months = birth of new religious order)

### 4. Faure's Four Cave Cult Types

**Typology** (pp. 150–151):

| Type | French | Example Sites | Function |
|------|--------|---------------|----------|
| **1** | *cultes d'initiations militaires* | Idaean Cave | Warrior initiation, Kouretes |
| **2** | *cultes courotrophiques* | Amnisos | Child-rearing deities (Eileithyia) |
| **3** | *cultes pastoraux* | Patsos | Hermes, Pan, Nymphs |
| **4** | *cultes chthoniens* | Various | Demeter-Kore, Dionysos |

**Relevance**: Skotino may have combined multiple cult types—a "labyrinth" in the functional sense of hosting diverse ritual activities in its interconnected chambers.

### 5. Cult Continuity and the "Corporation of Artisan-Magicians"

**Faure on the Dactyles and Kouretes** (p. 170):

> "le rôle d'initiateur mythique que nous avons vu dévolu aux Dactyles, puis aux Courètes de l'Ida, et que, par conséquent, une **corporation d'artisans magiciens** (tailleurs de pierre ou forgerons, peu importe) intervenait dans les mystères célébrés près de Knosos, en l'honneur d'Ariadne et du dieu à figure de taureau"
>
> (the role of mythical initiator that we have seen assigned to the Dactyles, then to the Kouretes of Ida, and that, consequently, a **corporation of artisan-magicians** (stone-cutters or smiths, it matters little) participated in the mysteries celebrated near Knossos, in honor of Ariadne and the bull-faced god)

**Bezalel-Daedalus parallel**: The Dactyles/Kouretes as a "corporation of artisan-magicians" mirrors the thesis that Daedalus represents a Minoan version of the Bezalel craftsman-priest archetype—the master artisan who builds the sanctuary and crafts the cult images.

### 6. Gaza's "Seigneur Marnas"—Cretan-Aramaic Connection

**Faure** (p. 159):

> "Victimes d'analogies extérieures, les habitants de Gaza, à l'époque romaine, donnaient inversement des origines crétoises à leur «Seigneur» Marnas, au nom purement araméen."
>
> (Victims of external analogies, the inhabitants of Gaza, in the Roman period, inversely gave Cretan origins to their "Lord" Marnas, [a god] with a purely Aramaic name.)

**Significance**: This establishes a documented tradition of **Cretan-Levantine religious exchange**. The Gaza cult of Marnas claimed Cretan origins, supporting the thesis of cultural transmission between Crete and the Semitic world. (See Part III: [meinos-manna-etymology.md](../part-3-minos/meinos-manna-etymology.md) for the Minos-Marnas connection.)

### 7. The Phoenician Connection in Britomartis's Genealogy

**Antoninus Liberalis 40**:

> "Kassiepeia, daughter of Arabios, and **Phoinix, son of Agenor**, had a daughter Karme. Zeus made love to her and fathered Britomartis... First she arrived in Argos from **Phoinikia**..."

This genealogy places Britomartis within the **Phoenician family of Europa and Kadmos**, suggesting awareness of Near Eastern origins for the Cretan goddess.

### Primary Sources

- KN X 140: *da-pu₂-ri-to* (probable reference to labyrinth)
- KN Gg 702: *da-pu₂-ri-to-jo po-ti-ni-ja me-ri* ("To the Lady of the Labyrinth: honey")

**Callimachus, *Hymn to Artemis* 188ff**: Nine-month pursuit narrative

**Pausanias 9.39.3**: Daedalus's statue of Britomartis at Olous

**Strabo 10.4.12–13**: Diktynnaion temple on Mt. Tityros

**Antoninus Liberalis 40**: Britomartis's Phoenician genealogy

### Scholarly Support

**Faure, Paul** (*Fonctions des Cavernes Crétoises*, 1964): Skotino as the original Labyrinth; Britomartis identification; four-fold cave cult typology.

**Evans, Arthur** (*Palace of Minos* I, p. 163): Noted Skotino's candidacy as the sacred cave of Knossos.

**Willetts, R.F.** (*Cretan Cults and Festivals*, 1962): Standard reference on Cretan religious traditions.

**Marinatos, Nanno** (*Minoan Religion*, 1993): Priestess impersonation of the goddess; "strong corporation" of priestly craftsmen.
