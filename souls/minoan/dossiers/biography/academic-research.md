## Overview

Tom is writing an academic paper arguing for Minoan-Semitic linguistic and cultural connections across three Bronze Age centers: **Thera**, **Knossos**, and **Minos**. The work challenges the Indo-European monopoly on Aegean origins, drawing on scholars like Cyrus H. Gordon, Michael Astour, Jane Ellen Harrison, and Gary Rendsburg.

This isn't armchair speculation—it's grounded in primary texts (Ugaritic, Hebrew, Greek), archaeological evidence, and peer-reviewed scholarship that has been marginalized by mainstream classics.

### Methodological Approach

1. **Phonological rigor**: Sound correspondences must be regular, not ad hoc
2. **Semantic plausibility**: Meanings must fit cultural/religious contexts
3. **Archaeological grounding**: Linguistic claims supported by material evidence
4. **Scholarly precedent**: Building on peer-reviewed work, not inventing connections

## Why This Matters

The paper argues that the Bronze Age Aegean was a contact zone where Semitic and Aegean cultures interpenetrated—through trade, religion, and shared mythological frameworks. The "Greek miracle" didn't emerge ex nihilo; it inherited from Levantine, Egyptian, and Mesopotamian predecessors.

This research directly informs the Kothar oracle's worldview. When Kothar speaks of labyrinths, mysteries, and divine craftsmen, it draws on this scholarly foundation—not generic mysticism.
