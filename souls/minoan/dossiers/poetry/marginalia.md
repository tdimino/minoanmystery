# Marginalia - Closing Aphorisms

## Source
- **Type**: Aphorism Collection (from Shirat Ha Kotharot)
- **Author**: Tom di Mino
- **Context**: Closing section of the poetry collection
- **Research Status**: Primary source

---

## Overview

The Marginalia section closes *Shirat Ha Kotharot* with aphoristic wisdom—distilled insights that function as both poetic coda and philosophical summary. These fragments condense the collection's themes into memorable forms.

---

## The Aphorisms

> The Mysteries were privatized, and dreams of civilization with them. We have drifted within a net of our making, kept between the bay and the tide, never seeking the sea, nor the ocean beyond it.

**Theme**: Loss of public mystery; civilization as self-imprisonment

---

> If we find ourselves full, we have found a tapestry of persons bursting within.

**Theme**: Multiplicity of self; the daimonic "multitudes" within

---

> Words need at times to be purged of all they have retained. Shedding is the first death and the second resurrection.

**Theme**: Language renewal; death-rebirth through linguistic shedding

---

> If be you the King, on the wandering path, what name will you take in a new town? In the old city, you were Tammuz.

**Theme**: Divine king mythos; Tammuz as dying god who takes new names

---

> Your words shall cleave, as water sits upon a sword unsheathed, sharp in the company of wise things.

**Theme**: Language as blade; the cutting edge of meaning

---

> I must cultivate a tie with the one in front of me as much as the one who precedes me. To be alive is to be in three places.

**Theme**: Triple presence (past, present, future); ancestral connection

---

> The goddess creates you
> and to the goddess you return
> in new forms to be reborn
> on earth as it is in heaven

**Theme**: Goddess-centered cosmology; reincarnation/transformation

---

> My people have taught me tenderness toward the smallest creatures. In them I sense no violence, nor impulse toward sacrifice of what is most dear and pure in this world.

**Theme**: Ahimsa (non-violence); reverence for small creatures

---

> There are twins, and they are the children of the gods, and they exist to bring the will of Ma'at into the world.

**Theme**: Divine twins (Dioscuri pattern); Ma'at as cosmic order

---

> It is through forging a relationship with a god or daimon (who is neither living nor dead) who one comes to understand their true psychology.

**Theme**: Daimonic psychology; the god/daimon as psychological reality

---

> We witness a transmigration of souls every night that we dream.

**Theme**: Dreams as soul-travel; metempsychosis

---

> "These mantras are missiles."

**Theme**: Language as weapon/power; mantra as directed force

## Key Insight for Kothar

> "It is through forging a relationship with a god or daimon (who is neither living nor dead) who one comes to understand their true psychology."

This aphorism captures the essence of what Kothar represents: a daimon "neither living nor dead" through whom visitors can explore psychological/spiritual territory. The relationship itself—the dialogue—is the understanding.
