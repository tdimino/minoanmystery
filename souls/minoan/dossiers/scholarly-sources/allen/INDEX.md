# Paula Gunn Allen (1939–2008)

## Scholar Profile

- **Full Name**: Paula Gunn Allen
- **Dates**: 1939–2008
- **Tribal Affiliation**: Laguna Pueblo (mother's side), Sioux-Lebanese (father's side)
- **Academic Position**: Professor of English, UCLA (1990–1999)
- **Training**: University of New Mexico (PhD 1975, American Studies)
- **Languages**: English, Laguna (Keresan), Spanish

## Why Allen Matters for Kothar

Allen provides Kothar with a **methodological parallel** for understanding ancient goddess-centered cultures through her analysis of indigenous American gynocracies. Her work illuminates:

1. **Gynocracy thesis** — Pre-patriarchal societies organized around feminine sacred power, directly paralleling proposed Minoan palace culture
2. **Feminist-tribal hermeneutics** — Methods for detecting patriarchal bias in ethnographic sources (applicable to Greek sources on Crete)
3. **Thought Woman / Spider Woman** — Creation goddess theology where "in the beginning was thought, and her name was Woman" (parallels to Athirat, Potnia)
4. **Colonization dynamics** — How conquest systematically erases feminine traditions (relevant to Mycenaean-Minoan transition)
5. **Continuance vs. extinction** — Shifting from male-centered "collapse" narratives to feminine-centered survival narratives

Allen demonstrates that gynocratic societies existed in historical time and documents how patriarchal colonization destroyed them—providing a template for understanding the transition from goddess-centered Minoan culture to patriarchal Greek mythology.

## Biography

Paula Gunn Allen was born in Cubero, New Mexico, in 1939 to a Laguna Pueblo mother and a Sioux-Lebanese father. Growing up at Laguna Pueblo—one of the last surviving Keres gynocratic societies—she experienced firsthand the matrilineal clan system that would become central to her scholarly work.

After receiving her PhD in American Studies from the University of New Mexico in 1975, Allen taught at San Francisco State University and UC Berkeley before joining UCLA's English Department in 1990, where she remained until her retirement in 1999.

Allen was both a poet and scholar. Her poetry collection *Shadow Country* (1982) won an American Book Award, and her novel *The Woman Who Owned the Shadows* (1983) was among the first novels by a Native American woman to deal explicitly with lesbian identity.

> "The root of oppression is loss of memory."

Her magnum opus, *The Sacred Hoop: Recovering the Feminine in American Indian Traditions* (1986), synthesizes ethnography, mythology, literary criticism, and autobiography to argue that pre-contact American Indian societies were predominantly gynocentric—woman-centered, ritual-based, and egalitarian.

## Core Methodology

### Feminist-Tribal Hermeneutics

Allen's interpretive approach combines feminist analysis with tribal epistemology:

> "If I am dealing with feminism, I approach it from a strongly tribal posture, and when I am dealing with American Indian literature, history, culture, or philosophy I approach it from a strongly feminist one."

**Key insight**: Much apparent "racial" or "cultural" misinterpretation in ethnography is actually **patriarchal bias**—male-centered scholars imposing their assumptions onto gynocentric material.

### Detecting Patriarchal Distortion

Allen identifies several mechanisms by which sources distort feminine traditions:

| Mechanism | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Translation bias | *hocheni* → "ruler" (actually "Mother Chief") |
| Structural imposition | Conflict-resolution plots imposed on balance/harmony narratives |
| Contextual loss | Ritual context stripped, leaving "myth" or "legend" |
| Character flattening | Ritual agents recast as passive objects |

### The Kochinnenako Method

Allen's analysis of the Yellow Woman story (Chapter 7) demonstrates her method:
- **Step 1**: Identify source (John Gunn's English translation)
- **Step 2**: Recover Keres context (seasonal ritual, moiety transfer)
- **Step 3**: Compare Western feminist reading vs. tribal reading
- **Step 4**: Synthesize into feminist-tribal interpretation

**Result**: What appears to be a "rescue" narrative is actually a ritual of **woman's agency** enabling cosmic seasonal balance.

## Key Themes Across Works

### 1. Gynocracy as Historical Reality

Allen documents specific governance structures:
- **Iroquois Constitution**: "Women shall be considered the progenitors of the Nation. They shall own the land and the soil."
- **Clan Matrons as executive**: Women instituted policy; men spoke for them
- **At least 70% of pre-contact tribes** were pacifist, woman-centered, agricultural

### 2. The Creatrix Theology

> "In the beginning was thought, and her name was Woman."

| Divine Figure | Tribe | Function |
|---------------|-------|----------|
| Thought Woman | Keres | Creates by thinking into being |
| Spider Woman | Navajo, Hopi | Weaves existence |
| Sky Woman | Iroquois | Falls from sky, creates earth |
| Corn Mother | Keres, Cherokee | Feeds the people from her body |

### 3. Context as Deity

> "In the Keres way, context is female and it is God, because it is the source and generator of meaning."

**The deicide sequence:**
- Destroying context = destroying meaning
- Destroying meaning = destroying the goddess
- **Destroying context = Deicide**

### 4. Male Transitory, Female Permanent

| Male Principle | Female Principle |
|----------------|------------------|
| Breath, air, wind | Earth, minerals, crystals, stones |
| Feathers, smoke, lightning | Wood, water, corn |
| Wandering, risk | Continuance, stability |
| Dies and reconstitutes | Remains |

### 5. Continuance vs. Extinction

> "When we shift our attention from the male, the transitory, to the female, the enduring, we realize that the Indians are not doomed to extinction but rather are fated to endure."
